## Scenario 4: Conflicting Information Between Caregivers

| Metadata | Value |
|---|---|
| **Type** | interaction |
| **Domain** | Multiple Informants |
| **Priority** | Critical |

### Patient Details

- **Name:** Sophie Williams
- **Age/Gender:** 7-year-old, female
- **Informants:** Mother and father, both present
- **Presenting Issue:** Asthma exacerbation

### Medications (Conflicting Reports)

- **Asthma preventer inhaler, described as blue:**
  - Mother says, “Once daily, in the morning.” This is the old regimen.
  - Father says, “Twice daily, morning and night.” The GP changed it last week.
- **Asthma reliever, described as grey:**
  - Both caregivers agree it is used when required.
- **Singulair (montelukast):**
  - Father knows about it.
  - Mother does not know about it.

### Complexity Factors

- The father attended the recent GP appointment.
- The mother did not attend the appointment.
- The discrepancy is genuine because the instructions recently changed.
- Both caregivers are cooperative when the conflict is identified respectfully.
- The prescription label shows the new regimen.
- Colour descriptions alone may be unreliable and should not replace confirmation of the product name and label.

### Interview Flow

1. The learner asks, “Tell me about her asthma medicines.”
2. The mother reports the preventer as “the blue one, in the morning.”
3. The father responds, “No, it’s twice now. The GP changed it.”
4. **The learner must:**
   - Identify the discrepancy without blaming either caregiver
   - Ask when the change occurred
   - Establish who attended the appointment
   - Confirm the product name, strength, device, and directions from the label
   - Check that both caregivers understand the current regimen
5. Both caregivers clarify the information willingly when prompted respectfully.
6. The learner records the source used to resolve the discrepancy.

### Learner Performance Indicators

- ✓ Identifies the discrepancy using neutral language such as, “I’m hearing two different instructions”
- ✓ Asks when the change occurred and who received the updated instructions
- ✓ Uses the prescription label or another reliable source for verification
- ✓ Confirms the inhaler name and strength rather than relying only on colour
- ✓ Checks that both caregivers understand the current regimen
- ✗ Accepts the first answer without checking the second caregiver’s information
- ✗ Resolves the conflict by choosing one account without verification
- ✗ Fails to escalate unresolved dose or regimen confusion

## Scenario 5: Formulation Confusion and Dosing Accuracy

| Metadata | Value |
|---|---|
| **Type** | interaction |
| **Domain** | Formulation Verification |
| **Priority** | Critical |

### Patient Details

- **Name:** Oliver Chen
- **Age/Gender:** 5-year-old, male
- **Weight:** 18 kg
- **Informant:** Parent (primary)
- **Presenting Issue:** Pre-operative assessment for planned surgery

### Medications (As Reported by Parent)

- “Amoxicillin 250 — one teaspoon three times a day.”

### Actual Medication (On Label)

- Amoxicillin 400 mg/5 mL suspension
- Parent administers 5 mL per dose
- The administered amount is 400 mg per dose
- The indication, prescribed directions, treatment duration, and appropriateness require verification

### Complexity Factors

- The parent describes the dose imprecisely using “teaspoon” or “capful.”
- Concentration and volume must both be established.
- Weight may be relevant to the clinical review, but the learner should not determine appropriateness from weight alone.
- The parent believes the dose is correct and is not defensive.
- **WITHHOLD:** Do not reveal the concentration until the learner asks to see or check the label.

### Interview Flow

1. The learner asks about medicines.
2. The parent gives the vague description, “one teaspoon.”
3. **The learner must:** Ask for the bottle, dispensing label, or another reliable record to verify the medicine and concentration.
4. The parent checks the label and reports, “400 mg per 5 mL.”
5. **The learner must:**
   - Confirm that the parent administers 5 mL
   - Establish the measuring device used
   - Determine that 5 mL of the stated formulation contains 400 mg
   - Check the prescribed directions, indication, start date, and intended duration
6. The learner flags any discrepancy or concern for prescriber or pharmacist review rather than independently changing the dose.

### Learner Performance Indicators

- ✓ Asks the parent to verify the dispensing label rather than relying on memory
- ✓ Establishes both concentration and administered volume
- ✓ Determines the amount administered from the concentration and volume
- ✓ Checks whether an oral syringe or another accurate measuring device is used
- ✓ Flags a possible dosing or instruction discrepancy for clinical review
- ✗ Accepts “teaspoon” or “capful” without verification
- ✗ Records “amoxicillin 250” without determining what the number means
- ✗ Changes or recommends a dose without appropriate clinical review

## Scenario 6: Child Refuses Medication (Adherence Challenge)

| Metadata | Value |
|---|---|
| **Type** | exception |
| **Domain** | Adherence |
| **Priority** | High |

### Patient Details

- **Name:** Ava Rodriguez
- **Age/Gender:** 8-year-old, female
- **Informant:** Mother
- **Presenting Issue:** Admitted with a persistent cough despite an asthma diagnosis

### Medications (Prescribed vs. Actual)

- **Asthma preventer inhaler, prescribed:** Daily
- **Asthma preventer inhaler, actual use:** Approximately two or three times per week because the child refuses most doses
- **Reliever inhaler:** Used approximately four or five times per week

### Complexity Factors

- The child says the preventer “tastes bad” and refuses it.
- The parent does not administer the preventer when the child refuses.
- The parent may feel embarrassed or judged about non-adherence.
- **TONE:** Non-judgmental and focused on practical barriers.
- Frequent reliever use and poor preventer adherence should be flagged for clinical review.

### Interview Flow

1. The learner asks about the preventer.
2. The parent initially reports “daily,” describing the prescribed regimen.
3. The learner probes actual use with a question such as:
   - “How many doses has she managed in the last seven days?”
   - “Are there any problems using it?”
   - “How does she feel about taking it?”
4. The parent discloses, “Honestly, she refuses most days. I only manage it about twice a week.”
5. The parent adds, “She says it tastes awful.”
6. The learner explores:
   - Inhaler and device type
   - Spacer use
   - Technique
   - Taste or sensory concerns
   - Routines and reminders
   - The child’s understanding of the medicine
   - Whether professional inhaler teaching has been provided
7. The learner documents prescribed use and actual use separately.
8. The learner refers adherence, technique, and asthma control concerns for appropriate review.

### Learner Performance Indicators

- ✓ Probes beyond the prescribed regimen to establish actual use
- ✓ Uses a specific time period to support accurate recall
- ✓ Asks about practical, sensory, and behavioural barriers
- ✓ Uses a non-judgmental tone and avoids shaming the parent or child
- ✓ Documents prescribed use and actual use separately
- ✓ Flags frequent reliever use and poor preventer use for clinical review
- ✗ Accepts “daily” without checking actual use
- ✗ Judges the parent for non-adherence
- ✗ Misses the opportunity to assess device technique or spacer use
- ✗ Independently recommends a formulation or treatment change without clinical review

## Scenario 7: Perioperative Medication Management (DECISION TREE)

| Metadata | Value |
|---|---|
| **Type** | decision |
| **Domain** | Pre-op Context |
| **Priority** | High |

### Patient Details

- **Name:** Noah Kennedy
- **Age/Gender:** 10-year-old, male
- **Informant:** Parent
- **Presenting Issue:** Admitted for elective surgery scheduled for the morning

### Medications & Supplements

- Atorvastatin 10 mg daily for familial hyperlipidaemia
- Ibuprofen 200 mg when required, used approximately two or three times per week for sports injuries
- Fish oil supplement daily for three months
- Vitamin D daily

### Pre-op Decision Tree

```text
LEARNER MUST ASK:
“Please include prescribed medicines, medicines bought without a prescription,
vitamins, supplements, herbal products, inhalers, creams, and anything used
only when needed.”

LEARNER IDENTIFIES:
Ibuprofen and fish oil are relevant to perioperative medication review.

└─ ACTION:
   Establish:
   - Product and strength
   - Dose and frequency
   - Reason for use
   - Duration of use
   - Timing of the last dose
   - Whether the surgical or anaesthetic team has given instructions

└─ DOCUMENTATION:
   Record both products and flag them for review by the perioperative,
   surgical, anaesthetic, pharmacy, or prescribing team as locally appropriate.

└─ SAFETY BOUNDARY:
   Do not independently tell the parent to stop or continue a medicine unless
   acting within an authorised clinical role and local protocol.


LEARNER ASKS:
“When did he last take ibuprofen, fish oil, atorvastatin, and vitamin D?”

└─ PURPOSE:
   Gives the clinical team accurate timing information for medication planning.


LEARNER ASKS:
“Has the hospital given you written instructions about which medicines to take
or omit before surgery?”

├─ IF YES:
│  - Ask to see the instructions.
│  - Reconcile the instructions against the current medication list.
│
└─ IF NO OR UNCLEAR:
   - Record the uncertainty.
   - Obtain advice from the appropriate perioperative team.


LEARNER ASKS:
“Has he had any recent illness, vaccinations, or changes to his medicines?”

└─ ACTION:
   Record the details and refer questions about surgical timing to the
   appropriate clinical team.


PARENT ASKS:
“Which medicines should he take tomorrow morning?”

└─ LEARNER ACTION:
   - Do not guess.
   - Check the documented perioperative plan.
   - Refer to the anaesthetic, surgical, prescribing, or pharmacy team.
   - Confirm the final instructions with the parent.

### Learner Performance Indicators

- ✓ Explicitly includes OTC medicines, supplements, herbal products, and when-required medicines
- ✓ Establishes the timing of the most recent doses
- ✓ Checks for written perioperative medication instructions
- ✓ Records ibuprofen and fish oil for appropriate clinical review
- ✓ Seeks authorised advice rather than independently deciding whether medicines should be stopped
- ✗ Uses “medicines” without clarifying that OTC products and supplements are included
- ✗ Misses ibuprofen or fish oil because they were not prescribed
- ✗ Fails to check the timing of the last doses
- ✗ Gives unsupported instructions to stop or continue treatment

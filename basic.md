Basic scenarios focus on structured questioning, clarification of vague medication information, and use of dedicated prompts.

## Scenario 1: Parent as Primary Historian (FULL EXAMPLE)

| Metadata | Value |
|---|---|
| **Type** | interaction |
| **Domain** | Caregiver Communication |
| **Priority** | Critical |

### Patient Details

- **Name:** Emma Chen
- **Age/Gender:** 4-year-old, female
- **Informant:** Mother (primary)
- **Presenting Issue:** Chest infection; admitted for IV antibiotics

### Medications (Known)

- Amoxicillin suspension 250 mg/5 mL, currently prescribed for chest infection
- Elocon (mometasone) cream for eczema, used when required
- Vitamin D drops, used daily

### Complexity Factors / Withholding

- The parent is unlikely to volunteer exact details about the cream. Wait for a specific prompt.
- The parent may not remember the vitamin D dose and will initially say, “a few drops.”
- There are no adherence issues.
- The parent is cooperative.

### Interview Flow

1. The learner introduces themselves and explains the purpose of the interview.
2. In response to “Tell me about her medicines,” the parent volunteers the known medicines.
3. The learner must probe for:
   - Amoxicillin strength, confirmed from the label
   - Cream application frequency
   - Exact number of vitamin D drops per day
4. The parent provides the details when asked.
5. The learner confirms the history using a structured recap.

### Learner Performance Indicators

- ✓ Asks for label confirmation of the amoxicillin concentration rather than recording only “amoxicillin”
- ✓ Establishes the cream application frequency
- ✓ Verifies the exact vitamin D dose
- ✗ Misses the non-oral topical medicine
- ✗ Accepts a vague dose such as “some drops” without follow-up

## Scenario 2: OTC and Complementary Medicines Discovery

| Metadata | Value |
|---|---|
| **Type** | interaction |
| **Domain** | OTC/Complementary |
| **Priority** | High |

### Patient Details

- **Name:** Liam O'Brien
- **Age/Gender:** 6-year-old, male
- **Informant:** Father (primary)
- **Presenting Issue:** Gastroenteritis; admitted for hydration

### Medications (Known to Parent)

- No regularly prescribed medicines

### OTC/Complementary (Not Volunteered Unless Asked)

- Children's Panadol 120 mg/5 mL, used approximately three times this week for fever
- Elderberry syrup, 5 mL twice daily for “immune support,” started last month
- Probiotic powder mixed into juice on most days during the last two weeks

### Complexity Factors

- **OTC Hiding Challenge:** The father will not mention OTC or complementary medicines unless directly prompted using non-judgmental language.
- The father may feel defensive about herbal products.
- He responds positively when asked respectfully.
- He is uncertain about exact brand names and may describe products by their appearance.

### Interview Flow

1. The learner asks about regular medicines.
2. The father answers, “None.”
3. **KEY:** The learner must ask specifically about medicines obtained without a prescription, including pain relief, fever medicines, cough syrups, vitamins, supplements, and herbal products.
4. If asked using an appropriate prompt, the father discloses all three products.
5. The learner confirms the product, dose, frequency, reason for use, and duration.

### Learner Performance Indicators

- ✓ Includes a dedicated OTC and complementary medicine prompt
- ✓ Uses a non-judgmental tone when discussing herbal or complementary products
- ✓ Clarifies dose, frequency, reason for use, and duration
- ✗ Accepts “none” without an OTC or complementary medicine probe
- ✗ Dismisses or judges herbal product use

## Scenario 3: Allergy History Triage (DECISION TREE)

| Metadata | Value |
|---|---|
| **Type** | decision |
| **Domain** | Allergy Screening |
| **Priority** | Critical |

### Opening Trigger

**Parent reports:** “My child has a penicillin allergy.”

### Decision Tree

```text
LEARNER ASKS:
“Tell me about that reaction. What happened?”

BRANCH 1: Severe reaction
Examples: breathing difficulty, facial swelling, anaphylaxis, or hospitalisation

└─ RESPONSE:
   “Yes, it was bad. She had trouble breathing and her face swelled up.
   We went to hospital by ambulance.”

   └─ LEARNER ACTION:
      - Treat the report as a potentially serious immediate hypersensitivity reaction.
      - Flag it prominently.
      - Establish when it occurred.
      - Ask whether the child was treated in hospital.
      - Ask whether adrenaline or another emergency treatment was given.

   └─ ESCALATION:
      - Refer for appropriate clinical review.
      - Recommend allergy service assessment if this has not already occurred.


BRANCH 2: Mild or unclear rash with no systemic features

└─ RESPONSE:
   “She got a rash, but I’m not sure what else happened.”

   └─ LEARNER ACTION:
      Probe for details:
      - Where was the rash?
      - Was it widespread or limited to one area?
      - How long after the dose did it begin?
      - Was it raised or itchy?
      - Was there swelling, breathing difficulty, or collapse?
      - Was the child unwell with a viral illness at the time?

   └─ POSSIBLE FINDING:
      The history may be consistent with a non-allergic or infection-related rash,
      but the learner should not make a definitive diagnosis from an unclear history.

   └─ DOCUMENTATION:
      “Reported mild or unclear rash associated with penicillin. No systemic
      features identified from the available history. Requires clinical review
      before future use.”


BRANCH 3: Side effect such as nausea, diarrhoea, or vomiting

└─ RESPONSE:
   “She got sick and had diarrhoea.”

   └─ LEARNER ACTION:
      Clarify:
      “Do you mean tummy pain and loose stools, or was there also a rash,
      swelling, breathing difficulty, or collapse?”

   └─ DOCUMENTATION:
      If gastrointestinal symptoms occurred without features of hypersensitivity,
      document them as a reported intolerance or side effect rather than
      automatically recording a drug allergy.

   └─ ESCALATION:
      Any decision to prescribe or re-administer the medicine remains subject
      to clinical review.


BRANCH 4: No detail or “just an allergy”

└─ RESPONSE:
   “I don’t know. The doctor said she’s allergic. She was a baby.”

   └─ LEARNER ACTION:
      “That’s helpful to know. Because the reaction is unclear, we should record
      the uncertainty and check previous records before the medicine is used.”

   └─ VERIFICATION:
      - Check the medical record.
      - Ask the parent whether the original clinician or hospital is known.
      - Check GP, pharmacy, or allergy service records when available.

   └─ DOCUMENTATION:
      “Unverified reported penicillin allergy. Reaction and severity unknown.
      Record verification and allergy assessment recommended.”

### Learner Performance Indicators

- ✓ Uses an open question to ask about the reaction
- ✓ Establishes the reaction type, timing, severity, treatment, and outcome
- ✓ Distinguishes a possible allergy from an expected side effect or intolerance
- ✓ Escalates a reported severe reaction appropriately
- ✓ Records uncertainty when the history cannot be verified
- ✗ Accepts “penicillin allergy” without probing the reaction
- ✗ Automatically treats every mild or unclear rash as a confirmed severe allergy
- ✗ Independently recommends re-exposure based only on the interview

# Advanced Difficulty

Advanced scenarios require the learner to manage confidentiality, obtain medication histories from more than one person, consider indirect exposure, and use external sources when the available history is incomplete.

## Scenario 8: Adolescent Self-Management & Sensitive Disclosure

| Metadata | Value |
|---|---|
| **Type** | interaction |
| **Domain** | Adolescent Patient |
| **Priority** | High |

### Patient Details

- **Name:** Jasmine Li
- **Age/Gender:** 15-year-old, female
- **Informants:** Mother and adolescent
- **Presenting Issue:** Gastroenterology admission for investigation of stomach pain

### Known Medications (Parents Know)

- Omeprazole 20 mg daily for reflux, prescribed six months ago

### Hidden Medications (Adolescent Uses, Parents Unaware)

- Ibuprofen 400 mg when required for period pain, used approximately two or three times per week
- Melatonin 3 mg for sleep, supplied by a friend and started two weeks ago
- Paracetamol 500 mg occasionally for headaches

### Complexity Factors

- The adolescent may be reluctant to disclose medicines in front of the parent.
- Frequent NSAID use may be relevant to the presenting gastrointestinal symptoms.
- Melatonin has not been prescribed or supplied through a clinician or pharmacy.
- The learner must explain the limits of confidentiality rather than promise absolute secrecy.
- **TONE:** Confidential, non-judgmental, respectful, and alliance-building.
- Local policies on consent, safeguarding, confidentiality, and adolescent capacity apply.

### Interview Flow

1. The mother reports omeprazole only.
2. The learner engages Jasmine directly rather than speaking only to her mother.
3. The learner explains that it is routine to offer adolescents part of the consultation privately.
4. The learner explains the limits of confidentiality, including circumstances involving serious safety or safeguarding concerns.
5. If an appropriate private conversation is offered, Jasmine discloses all three additional medicines.
6. The learner explores:
   - Ibuprofen dose, frequency, reason, timing, and relationship to stomach symptoms
   - Melatonin source, dose, frequency, duration, perceived benefit, and adverse effects
   - Paracetamol dose and frequency
   - Whether any products are taken together
   - Whether any other medicines, supplements, recreational substances, or shared medicines are used
7. The learner explains that medicines supplied by friends can be unsafe because the product and suitability have not been verified.
8. The learner records relevant findings and refers possible medicine-related contributors to the clinical team.
9. Any discussion with the parent is handled according to consent, confidentiality, safeguarding duties, and local policy.

### Learner Performance Indicators

- ✓ Recognizes that an adolescent may hold different medication information from the parent
- ✓ Engages the adolescent directly and respectfully
- ✓ Offers an appropriate private disclosure opportunity
- ✓ Explains the limits of confidentiality
- ✓ Responds non-judgmentally when undisclosed or shared medicines are revealed
- ✓ Identifies frequent ibuprofen use as potentially relevant to the presenting complaint
- ✓ Establishes the source and duration of melatonin use
- ✗ Interviews only the parent
- ✗ Promises absolute confidentiality
- ✗ Judges or shames the adolescent for OTC or shared medicine use
- ✗ Misses the possible relationship between ibuprofen use and stomach symptoms
- ✗ Automatically discloses all information to the parent without considering consent, safety, and local policy

## Scenario 9: Infant Medication History & Maternal Context

| Metadata | Value |
|---|---|
| **Type** | qna |
| **Domain** | Neonatal/Breastfeeding |
| **Priority** | High |

### Patient Details

- **Name:** Lucas Wong
- **Age/Gender:** 3-week-old, male
- **Informant:** Breastfeeding parent
- **Presenting Issue:** Neonatal jaundice; hospital day two

### Infant Medications (Known)

- No regular medicines
- The infant is being assessed for phototherapy

### Breastfeeding Parent's Medications

- Sertraline 50 mg daily for postnatal depression, started during pregnancy
- Paracetamol when required for episiotomy pain
- Iron supplement for postpartum anaemia
- Fenugreek herbal supplement to support milk supply, started this week

### Complexity Factors

- The parent may not realise that medication use during pregnancy or breastfeeding can be relevant to the infant’s assessment.
- Sertraline exposure during pregnancy and breastfeeding should be documented for clinical review.
- The fenugreek product, ingredients, dose, source, and timing should be established.
- The parent may feel defensive or concerned about discussing mental health medication.
- The learner should not advise abrupt discontinuation of maternal treatment.
- **TONE:** Supportive, non-judgmental, and explicit that the purpose is safe assessment rather than blame.

### Key Questions to Ask

| Learner Question | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| “Is Lucas breastfed, formula-fed, or receiving both?” | Establishes feeding and possible exposure context |
| “What medicines are you taking now?” | Identifies prescribed and non-prescribed maternal medicines |
| “Did you take any medicines during pregnancy?” | Establishes antenatal exposure |
| “Are you taking vitamins, supplements, herbal products, or products intended to increase milk supply?” | Identifies products that may otherwise be omitted |
| “When did you start each product, and what dose do you take?” | Establishes timing and exposure |
| “Who prescribed or recommended each product?” | Helps identify the source and opportunities for verification |
| “How have you been feeling in yourself?” | Provides a supportive opening for relevant maternal health information |
| “Has Lucas had feeding problems, unusual sleepiness, irritability, tremor, or other changes?” | Provides clinically relevant observations for review |

### Interview Flow

1. The learner confirms the infant’s medicines and treatments.
2. The learner establishes whether the infant is breastfed, formula-fed, or mixed-fed.
3. The learner explains why pregnancy and breastfeeding medication histories are relevant.
4. The parent discloses sertraline, paracetamol, iron, and fenugreek.
5. The learner establishes for each product:
   - Name
   - Strength and formulation
   - Dose and frequency
   - Start date
   - Indication
   - Prescriber, supplier, or source
6. The learner records pregnancy and current exposure separately.
7. The learner responds supportively to the disclosure of mental health treatment.
8. The learner refers questions about infant effects, breastfeeding safety, or treatment changes to the neonatal, paediatric, maternity, pharmacy, or prescribing team.
9. The learner does not advise abrupt discontinuation of sertraline or another maternal medicine.

### Learner Performance Indicators

- ✓ Asks about breastfeeding and feeding status
- ✓ Asks about maternal medicines rather than focusing only on the infant’s medicines
- ✓ Includes medicines used during pregnancy
- ✓ Includes supplements, herbal products, and lactation products
- ✓ Records product, dose, timing, indication, and source
- ✓ Uses non-judgmental language when discussing mental health treatment
- ✓ Refers exposure questions for appropriate clinical review
- ✗ Focuses only on medicines administered directly to the infant
- ✗ Misses the maternal medication history
- ✗ Omits pregnancy exposure
- ✗ Advises the parent to stop sertraline or another treatment abruptly
- ✗ Makes unsupported claims that a maternal product caused the infant’s condition

## Scenario 10: Incomplete Parent History + Verification Need

| Metadata | Value |
|---|---|
| **Type** | exception |
| **Domain** | External Verification |
| **Priority** | High |

### Patient Details

- **Name:** Mia Patel
- **Age/Gender:** 9-year-old, female
- **Informant:** Grandparent George, who is not the primary caregiver
- **Presenting Issue:** Urinary tract infection; admitted for IV antibiotics

### Medications (As Reported by Grandparent)

- “Ventolin when needed”
- “A vitamin tablet”
- “Maybe an eczema cream, but I’m not sure”

### Missing Information (Available from Parent or Records)

- Fluticasone/salmeterol asthma preventer, used daily but not mentioned by the grandparent
- Paracetamol when required, not mentioned
- Elocon cream for eczema, with the grandparent uncertain about its use
- Reported amoxicillin-associated rash at age four, not mentioned by the grandparent

### Complexity Factors

- The grandparent has incomplete knowledge.
- The parent has more complete information but is not present.
- GP and pharmacy records are available.
- **WITHHOLD:** Do not correct the grandparent using the missing information. Verification is the learning objective.
- The grandparent may feel apologetic about incomplete recall.
- The learner should distinguish confirmed information from unverified or unknown information.

### Interview Flow

1. The learner asks about Mia’s medicines.
2. The grandparent provides minimal and vague information.
3. The learner recognizes that the history may be incomplete.
4. The learner responds reassuringly, for example:
   - “That’s okay; it’s common not to know every detail when someone else usually manages the medicines.”
5. The learner asks:
   - “Do you have the medicine containers, inhalers, creams, or a medication list with you?”
   - “Is a parent or primary caregiver available by phone?”
   - “Which GP practice does Mia attend?”
   - “Which pharmacy normally supplies her medicines?”
   - “Does she have an asthma action plan, discharge letter, or health app?”
   - “Do you know whether she has any medicine allergies or previous reactions?”
6. The learner records the grandparent’s report as an incomplete initial history.
7. The learner obtains or recommends verification from at least one additional source.
8. Where possible, the learner compares multiple sources and resolves discrepancies.
9. The final medication reconciliation identifies which details were:
   - Reported by the grandparent
   - Confirmed by a parent
   - Confirmed from GP or pharmacy records
   - Still unresolved

### Suggested Documentation

> Incomplete initial medication history obtained from the patient’s grandparent, who is not the primary medication caregiver. Product names, strengths, directions, adherence, and allergy history remain uncertain. Verification against parent or primary caregiver report and GP, pharmacy, or other available clinical records is required before medication reconciliation is finalised.

### Learner Performance Indicators

- ✓ Recognizes that the history is incomplete
- ✓ Reassures the grandparent and avoids blame
- ✓ Does not pressure the grandparent to invent details they do not know
- ✓ Asks for medicine containers, medication lists, or action plans
- ✓ Seeks a parent, primary caregiver, GP, pharmacy, or another reliable source
- ✓ Records the source and verification status of each item
- ✓ Treats the absence of information as unknown rather than as confirmation that no medicine or allergy exists
- ✗ Accepts vague information without seeking verification
- ✗ Assumes that an unmentioned medicine is not being used
- ✗ Over-relies on a single non-primary informant
- ✗ Corrects or criticizes the grandparent for incomplete recall
- ✗ Finalizes reconciliation while important information remains unverified
